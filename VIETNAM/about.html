Embrace Our Nature, Live Our Legacy

Vietnam is where nature tells stories, and every view feels like a masterpiece. Picture emerald waters gliding through Ha Long Bay, the golden glow of endless rice terraces in Sapa, or hidden caves whispering ancient secrets in Phong Nha. This is Vietnam: alive, vibrant, and breathtakingly real.

The Ministry of Tourism invites you to wander where misty mountains touch the skies and traditions run as deep as rivers. Adventure awaits in lush jungles, pristine beaches, and bustling cities steeped in culture.

Step into Vietnam and feel the heartbeat of Southeast Asia. Here, nature and heritage unite to create unforgettable moments. Dare to discover it, and let Vietnam leave its mark on your soul.

From Emerald Hills to Azure Waves

Vietnam offers a range of breathtaking destinations, from the emerald waters of Ha Long Bay to the golden sands of Phu Quoc. With its unique combination of natural wonders and rich cultural heritage, Vietnam promises unforgettable experiences for every traveler.

Start your journey in Ha Long Bay, a UNESCO World Heritage Site where limestone peaks rise dramatically from the sea, creating a landscape unlike any other. Whether you’re cruising on its tranquil waters or exploring hidden caves, Ha Long is an essential stop on your adventure through Vietnam.

Venture further to the rolling hills of Sapa or the serene beaches of Da Nang, where vibrant cultures blend with lush landscapes. Each destination, from the misty mountains to the pristine coastlines, offers its own piece of Vietnam’s natural beauty and charm.

Places That Tell Our Story

Da Nang

Nestled between the ancient charm of Hoi An and the imperial legacy of Hue, Da Nang stands out as a dynamic coastal city where modern life and natural wonders coexist. Visitors are drawn to its golden beaches, like My Khe and Non Nuoc, perfect for sunseekers and surfers alike. Towering nearby are the Marble Mountains, a cluster of limestone hills housing caves, pagodas, and panoramic viewpoints.


Da Nang is also home to architectural icons such as the Dragon Bridge, which breathes fire over the Han River, and the Golden Bridge, a surreal walkway held aloft by giant stone hands in the Ba Na Hills. The city itself offers vibrant nightlife, delicious seafood, and a growing arts and café culture that feels refreshingly youthful and open.


With its blend of urban energy, cultural depth, and natural majesty, Da Nang is more than just a stop—it’s a destination that shows the future of Vietnam, grounded in its heritage and reaching confidently outward.

Ha Long Bay


Ha Long Bay, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is one of Vietnam’s most breathtaking natural treasures. Its dramatic limestone islands rise from emerald-green waters, forming a surreal landscape that feels both ancient and timeless. Cruising the bay aboard a traditional junk boat reveals hidden caves, floating villages, and narrow channels where nature whispers through stone and mist. Adventurers can kayak between karsts or hike viewpoints for sweeping vistas.


At dawn, the bay shimmers under soft light, while sunset paints its waters gold and crimson. Visitors are drawn not only by the scenery but also by the quiet serenity that defines each journey. Whether drifting in silence or sharing fresh seafood on deck, Ha Long Bay offers moments of calm that linger long after the trip ends.


More than a destination, Ha Long Bay is an experience of wonder—a place where water, stone, and sky meet in perfect harmony. Every ripple tells a story, every shadow hides a secret, and every turn of the boat reveals a new marvel. This is Vietnam’s natural poetry, written in waves.

Hanoi


Hanoi, Vietnam’s capital, is a city of elegant chaos and deep cultural roots. With its tree-lined boulevards, ancient temples, and French colonial facades, Hanoi blends old-world charm with vibrant street life. In the Old Quarter, narrow streets hum with scooters, vendors, and the aroma of pho rising from sidewalk kitchens. Nearby, tranquil lakes and quiet pagodas offer moments of peace amid the bustle.


The city is a tapestry of history. From the Temple of Literature to the Ho Chi Minh Mausoleum, Hanoi tells stories through its architecture and public spaces. Water puppet shows, weekend night markets, and coffee culture add layers of daily life rich with tradition and rhythm. Whether sipping egg coffee in a tucked-away café or hearing the clang of temple bells, Hanoi engages every sense.


Hanoi is the soul of Vietnam—complex, poetic, and enduring. It rewards patience and curiosity, revealing its beauty not in grand gestures, but in subtle textures and lived-in corners. To walk Hanoi is to move between centuries and cultures, past and present, in a city that never stops feeling alive.

Sapa


Sapa, nestled in the Hoàng Liên Son mountains of northern Vietnam, is a land of misty peaks and vibrant culture. Known for its terraced rice fields carved into hillsides, Sapa offers some of the country’s most breathtaking landscapes. Trekking paths weave through valleys and mountain passes, connecting small villages where ethnic minority communities like the Hmong, Dao, and Tay have lived for generations.


Sapa’s cool climate and dramatic terrain make it ideal for outdoor adventure. Visitors can hike to Fansipan—the highest peak in Indochina—or stroll through lush valleys dotted with wooden homes and water buffalo. Local markets buzz with colorful textiles, handmade crafts, and traditional foods that reflect the diversity and resilience of the region’s hill tribes.


Sapa is more than a scenic retreat—it’s a window into a way of life rooted in nature and heritage. From fog-draped mornings to starry nights above the highlands, every moment feels steeped in calm and meaning. Whether you’re walking silent trails or sharing tea with villagers, Sapa leaves its mark slowly, through quiet beauty and authentic connection.

Ninh Binh


Ninh Binh, often called “Ha Long Bay on land,” is a landscape of dramatic limestone cliffs rising above emerald rice paddies and winding rivers. Located just a couple of hours from Hanoi, it offers a peaceful escape into Vietnam’s natural and spiritual heart. Visitors can glide down the Ngo Dong River by rowboat, passing through low cave tunnels and lush valleys framed by jagged karst formations.


Beyond the scenery, Ninh Binh is home to ancient temples and royal history. Hoa Lu, the former capital of Vietnam in the 10th century, holds relics of dynastic power nestled among forested hills. Nearby, the vast Bai Dinh Pagoda complex blends tradition with scale, offering panoramic views and spiritual serenity. The area’s calm rhythm and open landscapes make it ideal for cycling, photography, or quiet reflection.


Ninh Binh is Vietnam at its most tranquil and timeless. Here, water flows softly between limestone giants, and echoes of the past linger among ancient stones. It’s not just a place to visit—it’s a place to breathe deeply, slow down, and absorb the quiet majesty of Vietnam’s countryside.

Ancient City of Hue


Hue, once the capital of imperial Vietnam, is a city where history lingers in stone walls, moats, and moss-covered temples. Nestled along the Perfume River, Hue’s UNESCO-listed Imperial City is a sprawling complex of palaces, gates, and courtyards built by the Nguyễn Dynasty. Within its walls, the echoes of emperors, mandarins, and court musicians still seem to resonate in the silence of ancient halls.


The city is also home to elaborate royal tombs, each uniquely designed to reflect the personality of the ruler it honors. These mausoleums, scattered across the surrounding countryside, are works of art as much as places of rest. Pagodas rise over riverside cliffs, and dragon boats glide gently through the mist, offering a view of Vietnam’s regal past wrapped in quiet beauty.


Hue is Vietnam’s memory carved in stone. It invites you to walk slowly, to listen carefully, and to appreciate a time when power met poetry. Whether you’re watching incense curl in a temple or tracing carvings on weathered gates, Hue offers a serene, contemplative experience that honors heritage with dignity and grace.

Hoi An


Hoi An is Vietnam’s most atmospheric town—a preserved trading port where history glows beneath silk lanterns and golden walls. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, the city enchants with its tiled-roof houses, wooden bridges, and a delicate blend of Vietnamese, Chinese, and French influences. The Old Town’s narrow lanes lead past tailor shops, riverside cafés, and colorful temples, where tradition is part of daily life.


Every evening, the Thu Bon River reflects a sea of lanterns, floating with wishes and quiet wonder. The monthly full moon festival transforms the town into a candle-lit dream, while nearby villages showcase crafts like pottery, woodcarving, and silk weaving. Cooking classes, cycling tours, and boat rides offer visitors a hands-on glimpse of Hoi An’s charm beyond its postcard beauty.


Hoi An is a living memory wrapped in light. It’s not just beautiful—it’s immersive. From savoring cao lầu noodles in a hidden courtyard to watching lanterns drift downstream, Hoi An invites you to slow down and feel part of something timeless. It’s a place where history doesn’t just survive—it thrives.

Phong Nha – Ke Bang


Phong Nha – Ke Bang National Park is a world of underground wonder and lush jungle landscapes. Home to some of the largest and most spectacular caves on Earth, including the legendary Sơn Đoòng, this UNESCO World Heritage Site is an adventurer’s paradise. The park’s limestone mountains hide vast caverns, underground rivers, and surreal rock formations that feel almost otherworldly.


Visitors can explore caves like Phong Nha, Paradise, or Dark Cave by boat, zipline, or on foot, encountering chambers the size of cathedrals and crystal-clear pools beneath the earth. Above ground, the park is just as captivating—jungle trails lead to waterfalls, wildlife sightings, and panoramic views of one of Vietnam’s most pristine natural environments.


Phong Nha is raw nature at its most powerful. It’s a place where silence is vast, darkness holds beauty, and every step feels like discovery. Whether you’re deep in a cave or gazing at jungle-covered peaks, the sense of awe is constant. For those seeking nature, mystery, and scale—Phong Nha offers an unforgettable descent into the heart of the Earth.

Ho Chi Minh City


Ho Chi Minh City, formerly known as Saigon, is Vietnam’s largest and most electric metropolis. A city of motion, contrast, and constant reinvention, it hums with scooters, street food, and skyscrapers. Here, colonial-era architecture stands beside glass towers, and timeless temples are tucked between buzzing cafés and neon-lit alleyways. It’s fast, bold, and full of life.


The city holds stories in its streets—from the war-era relics at the Reunification Palace and War Remnants Museum to the incense-filled halls of Jade Emperor Pagoda. Markets like Bến Thành burst with color, flavor, and rhythm. Day or night, there’s always something happening: rooftop bars with skyline views, steaming bowls of hủ tiếu, or locals playing cards by the roadside.


Ho Chi Minh City is energy embodied. It doesn’t wait—it pulls you in. It’s a place where tradition and ambition coexist, where every crossing is a dance, and every moment feels alive. Whether you’re here for the chaos or the culture, the city promises one thing: you won’t forget it.

Mekong Delta


The Mekong Delta, known as Vietnam’s "rice bowl," is a lush, water-bound region where life flows along rivers and canals. Spread across a vast network of waterways, the delta is dotted with stilt houses, coconut palms, and floating markets that form a vibrant tapestry of rural life. Boats replace buses here, and the rhythm is dictated by the river’s gentle current.


Visitors can explore early-morning floating markets like Cái Răng, where traders in conical hats sell fruits, vegetables, and local specialties directly from their boats. Tranquil villages offer a glimpse into daily life—farming, fishing, weaving, and cooking passed down through generations. Sampan rides through shaded mangroves and cycling through fruit orchards reveal a slower, simpler Vietnam steeped in authenticity.


The Mekong Delta is Vietnam’s pastoral heart. It’s a place of quiet beauty, warm hospitality, and deeply rooted traditions. Whether you’re sipping coconut water under a thatched roof or drifting past rice fields in golden light, the delta invites you to slow down, look closer, and experience the peaceful soul of the south.

Stories in Stone, Spirit in Every Corner

Hoi An, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, blends Vietnamese, Chinese, and Japanese influences. Its preserved ancient streets reflect Vietnam’s vibrant trading history, making it a living piece of the past.

Visit Hanoi’s One Pillar Pagoda and the Bai Dinh Pagoda, home to thousands of Buddha statues. These spiritual sites, alongside the My Son Sanctuary, remind visitors of Vietnam’s devotion to faith and culture.

A Journey of Taste and Tradition

Vietnamese cuisine is more than just food—it’s a mirror of the country’s culture, history, and daily life. On every street corner, you’ll find dishes like phở, bún chả, or bánh mì, prepared fresh, fast, and full of flavor. These street eats are not just convenient—they’re rooted in local pride and tradition.


But beyond the plastic stools and charcoal grills lies another layer: the refined cuisine of Vietnam’s regions. In Huế, ancient imperial recipes still shape spicy and elegant dishes like bún bò Huế. In the Mekong Delta, coconut milk-rich curries and abundant river fish define a cuisine built on the land and water.


What ties it all together? A deep respect for balance—between herbs and heat, texture and taste, simplicity and complexity.


North, Central, and South each leave their mark. And now, so does the world: from Paris to New York, Vietnamese food is on the rise. Yet the most authentic experience is still here— served hot in a Hanoi alley or a coastal village in Da Nang.
